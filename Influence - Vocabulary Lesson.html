Spelling
How do we write it?
influence
in  ·  flu  ·  ence
Cover it up. Write it from memory. Then check.
Common errors: "influnce" (dropping the final e), "influance" (wrong vowel in the last syllable), "infuence" (dropping the l). The ending is always -ence, not -ance.
Etymology
Where does the word come from?
Origin
Latin — influentia, via Old French
From classical Latin influentia, meaning "a flowing in." Entered English in the 14th century. Originally used in astrology — celestial bodies were said to "flow" their power into human character and events.
in-
"Into" or "upon"
A Latin prefix meaning "into" or directed at something. You can see it in inject (push into), inspire (breathe into), infiltrate (flow into gradually).
fluere
"To flow"
A Latin verb meaning "to flow." The same root gives us fluid (flows easily), fluent (words flow smoothly), flux (in a state of flow), and affluent (flowing toward — originally meaning "abundant").
in (into) + fluere (to flow) = "a flowing in" — a force that flows into something and shapes it
Definition
What does it mean?
General meaning
influence
noun (also used as a verb: to influence)
The power to affect, change, or shape someone or something without using direct force or authority. When something has influence, it causes things to develop differently than they otherwise would have.
In Theatre Studies
The effect that a practitioner, theatrical style, or production element has on the creative choices of another practitioner or production — or the effect that specific performance choices have on an audience. In exam writing, identifying an influence requires naming what caused the change, what specifically changed, and why it matters.
Key idea Influence always involves change. The question is not just "who influenced who" — it is what specifically changed as a result, and how you can see that in the work.
Example sentences

Sample response
A stronger answer
Teacher asked
"Identify and explain an influence on this production."
Sample response
"The director's work shows the influence of Brecht's Epic Theatre. Direct address is used throughout the production, preventing the audience from becoming emotionally absorbed and positioning them instead as critical observers. The episodic structure reinforces this influence: self-contained scenes resist emotional escalation and invite the audience to analyse events rather than simply feel them. These are not incidental choices — they reflect a deliberate use of Brechtian influence to give the production a political and intellectual edge."
